Size the spa to the river, not the river to the spa.

This is a lottery project, not an investment recommendation: acquire an
existing Finnish mill or small-hydro site, restore the energy system, and build
a bathhouse whose annual electricity demand roughly fits the river's annual
generation. The hydro plant is not a miniature utility business. It is the
retreat's load-bearing room and the reason the architecture exists.

The traditions compose as operating logic rather than decoration. Rome supplies
the sequence of temperatures and rooms. Japan supplies wash-before-bathing,
quiet water, and the outdoor _rotenburo_ form. Finland supplies the central
sauna, the smoke-sauna ritual, winter, wood, and the river itself.

## Acquire the constraint

A greenfield dam is the wrong starting point. It is not categorically illegal,
but hydropower and dam works require a water permit, new hydropower is tightly
limited by environmental and conservation concerns, and current Finnish energy
guidance locates most small-hydro potential in modernising existing or
decommissioned plants.

The useful search object is therefore an old mill estate with a lawful water
economy already attached to it. The first filter is not a pretty riverbank. It
is a legible permit and property history.

The reference site has:

- an existing dam, mill race, and generation history;
- about 5 m of usable head and 6 m³/s of design flow;
- buildable land above the design flood level, road access, and a plausible
  grid connection;
- room for a bathhouse without putting guest circulation inside dam operations;
- no unresolved ownership of the water power; and
- an ecological path worth improving, rather than damage the project would
  have to deny.

An old permit is an asset, not a magic inheritance. Refurbishment, a new intake,
a material change in flow, a heat-exchanger circuit, or new river access may
still require a permit or amended conditions. Before buying, commission a
permit audit, dam-condition survey, hydrology study, flood review, sediment and
soil contamination screen, grid study, and an initial fisheries and habitat
assessment. A romantic mill can contain an expensive century of missing paper.

## One reference river

Hydro power is approximately:

`P = ρ × g × Q × H × η`

At 5 m head, 6 m³/s design flow, and 85% combined efficiency, that is about 250
kW. At a deliberately unheroic 59% annual capacity factor, it produces about
1.3 GWh per year, or 148 kW averaged over the whole year.

Those are reference numbers, not a site forecast. The decision needs several
years of hourly flow data, the actual permit's flow limits, residual-flow and
fish-passage obligations, turbine curves, outages, icing history, and
downstream water levels. Nameplate power is the easy number. Firm winter energy
is the valuable one.

The turbine follows the site. Reusing a sound low-head Kaplan or propeller
installation may beat replacing it. An Archimedes screw is narratively strong,
slow enough to see, and tolerant of debris, but it is not automatically the
best hydraulic, ecological, or civil-works answer. Make the machinery visible
only after the permit, fish-passage design, and whole-life cost choose it.

The seasonal mismatch survives every pleasant annual total. Finnish hydro can
be weak in late winter when heat demand is strongest. The retreat remains
grid-connected, imports during some cold and low-flow hours, and exports during
some wet low-load hours. Export is spill value, not the business model.

## A spa that fits 1.3 GWh

The reference retreat is deliberately small:

- a 1,200 m² conditioned bathhouse;
- 40,000 physical visits per year, about 110 per day on average;
- a Roman thermal path with a cool room, warm social room, hot room, and steam;
- two outdoor 41–42 °C baths, each about 14 m² at the water surface;
- one dependable central sauna, electrically heated for controllability;
- a separate wood-fired sauna and a smoke sauna opened for booked rituals;
- a 60-cover restaurant; and
- eight cabins, with overflow lodging supplied by nearby houses rather than a
  hotel wing.

An indicative annual electricity budget is:

| Load                                                | Annual electricity | Winter coincident peak |
| --------------------------------------------------- | -----------------: | ---------------------: |
| Heat-pump compressors and source pumps              |           0.52 GWh |                  90 kW |
| Pool circulation, ventilation, drying, and lighting |           0.28 GWh |                  55 kW |
| Central sauna and steam                             |           0.12 GWh |                  50 kW |
| Restaurant                                          |           0.15 GWh |                  45 kW |
| Eight cabins                                        |           0.13 GWh |                  25 kW |
| Site, treatment, and other loads                    |           0.07 GWh |                  15 kW |
| **Total**                                           |       **1.27 GWh** |             **280 kW** |

The peaks do not all arrive together; a plausible diversified site peak is
230–260 kW. Hydro might be making only 100–150 kW at that moment. The grid
connection and controls must cover the difference without pretending that
annual balance is hourly self-sufficiency.

With a plausible hourly profile, the hydro plant could directly serve roughly
70–80% of consumption, while imports and incidental exports settle the rest.
That percentage is a model target, not a claim about a site that does not yet
exist. Imported volume can be matched with a wind contract or a share of
off-site wind production. An on-site turbine is a later landscape and planning
decision, not a gap-filling ornament.

## The river heats the baths, indirectly

The composition works because electricity becomes useful low-temperature heat.
A closed glycol loop takes energy from the tailrace through oversized,
cleanable heat exchangers. Two staged water-source heat pumps deliver pool heat,
underfloor heating, ventilation reheat, and domestic hot water. The Roman
promise of warm stone underfoot is the actual low-temperature distribution
system.

The model allows about 1.5 GWh of delivered heat per year and a conservative
seasonal performance factor near 2.9, producing the 0.52 GWh compressor load in
the table. River water near 0 °C is the load-bearing engineering risk. Intake
screens, exchanger area, freezing margin, debris handling, minimum flow, and
cleaning access matter more than the heat pump brochure.

Use multiple machines rather than one heroic machine, a 30–50 m³ thermal buffer
for hourly shifting, and a roughly 200 kW reserve boiler. Pellet or locally
available wood chip fits the story and supplies cold peaks, outages, and
maintenance periods. A compact electric boiler is still the sensible final
reserve. The outdoor baths receive automated insulated covers whenever they are
closed; two uncovered hot pools can demand roughly 25–45 kW of heat on a windy
design night.

The pools do not contain river water. Public indoor and outdoor spa pools fall
under Finnish pool-water quality and monitoring rules. Treat and recirculate
them as public pools, then express the onsen influence through form, washing,
quiet, material, and ritual. Decide any mineral dosing with the municipal health
authority and the treatment designer rather than writing sulfur into the brand
before the chemistry works.

## The bathing story

The guest path should read without a plaque.

1. **Threshold and washing.** Changing rooms lead to seated wash stations. The
   first rule is clean bodies entering shared water.
2. **Tepidarium.** A warm, daylit social room slows the guest down. Hydronic
   stone floors make the energy system tactile.
3. **Caldarium.** The path compresses into hot water, radiant surfaces, and
   steam. This is Roman sequence, not a room full of replica columns.
4. **Frigidarium.** A controlled cold pool faces the moving river. A seasonal
   river or ice-hole entry can exist downstream only after current, ice,
   lighting, rescue, water-quality, and supervision design make it an operated
   activity rather than an attractive hazard.
5. **Rotenburo.** Two quiet outdoor baths sit above the tailrace, screened from
   operations and each other. Snow, steam, and audible water provide the image
   the rest of the project must earn.
6. **Sauna as the centre.** The everyday sauna is central, reliable, and easy to
   turn over. The wood sauna and smoke sauna are slower booked events, not
   baseload equipment pretending to be picturesque.

Call the result onsen-inspired, not an onsen. Finland has hot water and winter,
not a geothermal spring at this hypothetical mill.

## The lottery allocation

The earlier €12 million version is possible only if the dam, buildings, ground,
and permits are unusually kind. A more useful dream allowance is €15–16 million
in 2026 euros, before financing and with a real owner contingency.

| Capital item                                          | Concept allowance |
| ----------------------------------------------------- | ----------------: |
| Mill estate, rights, and acquisition due diligence    |        €0.8–1.4 M |
| Dam, hydro refurbishment, controls, and grid works    |        €1.2–2.0 M |
| Fish passage and river-habitat work                   |        €0.4–0.9 M |
| Bathhouse, pools, and fixed spa building services     |        €5.2–6.5 M |
| Outdoor baths, landscape, and safe river access       |        €0.6–0.9 M |
| Heat plant, source loop, storage, and controls        |        €0.5–0.8 M |
| Wood-sauna and smoke-sauna buildings                  |        €0.3–0.6 M |
| Eight cabins                                          |        €1.4–1.9 M |
| Restaurant fit-out                                    |        €0.4–0.7 M |
| Design, permits, surveys, escalation, and contingency |        €1.8–2.7 M |
| **Total range**                                       |  **€12.6–18.4 M** |

The dam is the dangerous row. A material safety defect, unknown foundation, bad
sediment, or permit condition can consume the whole contingency before anyone
chooses a tile.

## A destination business, not a power sale

Järvisydän is useful as category inspiration: its public offer combines spa,
accommodation, restaurants, and packages into a reason to travel. This model
borrows that stack, not its scale or financials.

A soft operating case looks like this:

| Revenue line                   | Working assumption                        | Annual revenue |
| ------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------- | -------------: |
| Spa admission                  | 36,000 paid-equivalent visits × €48       |        €1.73 M |
| Cabins                         | 8 × 365 × 60% occupancy × €260            |        €0.46 M |
| Restaurant                     | resident, spa, and destination demand     |        €0.90 M |
| Treatments and private rituals | smoke sauna, therapies, small groups      |        €0.30 M |
| **Total**                      | before package eliminations and discounts |    **€3.39 M** |

At roughly €2.8–3.0 million of payroll, food and beverage inputs, laundry,
maintenance, insurance, marketing, administration, purchased energy, and fuel,
the site might leave €0.4–0.6 million before debt service, tax, depreciation,
and major replacement reserves. Hydro's economic contribution is avoided
purchased energy and a coherent destination, not a heroic export line.

This is a low single-digit return on a fragile capital base. As an investment,
it is easy to defeat with a spreadsheet. As a way to spend part of a lottery
win on a place that can pay much of its own operating bill, it survives.

## Build in the order of uncertainty

**Phase 0 — find and disprove.** Search permit records and sale inventory. Kill
sites on title, water rights, hydrology, dam condition, ecology, flood level,
contamination, grid cost, or municipal planning before drawing the spa.

**Phase 1 — make the river measurable.** Acquire the site, complete permit work,
restore the dam and plant, improve fish passage, and instrument generation,
tailrace temperature, flow, and ice. Operate through at least one winter.

**Phase 2 — freeze the energy brief.** Replace the reference model with measured
hourly data. Set the heat-pump duty, backup system, thermal storage, pool area,
and grid capacity. If the river supports a smaller spa, build the smaller spa.

**Phase 3 — open the reason to visit.** Build the bathhouse, restaurant, central
sauna, and the first four cabins. Add the remaining cabins and occasional
saunas only after guest flow and staffing are real.

The project fails cleanly if the acquired hydro site remains a restored plant
and mill estate. It fails badly if a half-built bathhouse depends on water,
permits, or winter performance that the owner had only imagined.

## Sources and model boundary

The regulatory and sector anchors were checked on 19 July 2026:

- Finland's environmental administration lists hydropower use, dam construction,
  and water regulation among works requiring a
  [water permit](https://www.ymparisto.fi/fi/luvat-ja-velvoitteet/vesilupa-ja-ilmoitukset).
- Motiva's current
  [hydropower overview](https://www.motiva.fi/en/databank/hydropower-in-finland/?topic=hydropower)
  gives a typical 2–6 m head for small Finnish hydro, describes its stream
  impacts, and places most potential in modernisation and existing sites.
- The official
  [renewable-energy permit guide](https://www.ymparisto.fi/sites/default/files/documents/Uusiutuvan%20energian%20tuotantolaitosten%20lupamenettelyt%20ja%20muut%20hallinnolliset%20menettelyt.pdf)
  explains that significant modifications can require a water permit and uses
  a decommissioned small-hydro site as its worked example.
- The current [Water Act](https://www.finlex.fi/fi/lainsaadanto/2011/587)
  provides for fisheries obligations or fees where a water project harms fish
  stocks or fishing.
- The pool-water
  [decree 315/2002](https://www.finlex.fi/fi/lainsaadanto/2002/315) applies to
  public indoor and outdoor spa pools; Valvira's
  [application guide](https://valvira.fi/documents/152634019/163769316/Allasvesiasetuksen-soveltamisohje.pdf)
  is the practical starting point for treatment and monitoring discussions.
- Järvisydän's own site shows the
  [spa, lodging, restaurant, and package](https://www.jarvisydan.com/en/)
  structure used only as category inspiration here.

Everything else is a replaceable concept assumption: flow, capacity factor,
heat load, heat-pump performance, direct-consumption share, pool loss, visitor
count, room rate, occupancy, staffing, capex, and revenue. The arithmetic is
there to make the dream falsifiable, not bankable.
